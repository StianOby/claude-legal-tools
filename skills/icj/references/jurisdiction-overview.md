# ICJ jurisdiction — quick reference

Brief recap of the legal architecture. Read this when answering any
question about how the Court takes a case, or whether it can.

## Statute provisions

The Court is governed by its **Statute** (annexed to the UN Charter). The
relevant articles for jurisdiction are:

- **Article 34(1)** — Only states may be parties before the Court (no
  individuals, no corporations, no IGOs in contentious proceedings).
- **Article 35(1)** — The Court is open to states parties to the Statute.
- **Article 35(2)** — The Court may also be open to non-party states under
  conditions laid down by the Security Council (currently SC Resolution 9
  (1946); see `jurisdiction non-parties`).
- **Article 36(1)** — Jurisdiction comprises (a) all cases the parties
  refer to it, and (b) all matters specially provided for in the Charter or
  in treaties and conventions in force.
- **Article 36(2)** — The optional clause: states may "at any time declare
  that they recognize as compulsory ipso facto and without special agreement,
  in relation to any other State accepting the same obligation, the
  jurisdiction of the Court". This is the **compulsory jurisdiction**
  declaration system.
- **Article 36(5)** — Declarations made under the **PCIJ** Statute, still in
  force, are deemed acceptances of ICJ jurisdiction.
- **Article 36(6)** — In the event of a dispute as to jurisdiction, the
  Court itself decides ("compétence de la compétence").
- **Article 38** — Sources of international law the Court applies.
- **Article 65** — Advisory jurisdiction (only for organs/agencies
  authorised by the General Assembly under Charter Art. 96(2)).

## How a contentious case actually gets to the Court

A unilateral application or a special-agreement notification is filed.
Jurisdiction must rest on at least one of the following:

1. **Special agreement (compromis)** — both parties jointly request the
   Court's adjudication of a defined dispute. Examples: *Frontier Dispute
   (Burkina Faso/Mali)*, *Gabčíkovo-Nagymaros (Hungary/Slovakia)*.
2. **Compromissory clause in a treaty in force between the parties** —
   either party can invoke the clause unilaterally. The Court has
   jurisdiction only over disputes that fall within the wording of the
   clause. Examples: Genocide Convention Art. IX, Vienna Convention on
   Consular Relations Art. I of the Optional Protocol.
3. **Optional-clause declarations under Article 36(2)** — both states must
   have declarations on file, both must include the subject-matter of the
   dispute, and reciprocity applies (each can invoke the other's
   reservations). See `declaration-analysis.md`.
4. **Forum prorogatum** — consent expressed by conduct after proceedings
   have been started, e.g. by appearing and pleading without contesting
   jurisdiction. Rare but extant. Example: *Certain Criminal Proceedings
   in France (Republic of the Congo v. France)*, where France gave express
   consent in a letter.

## Reciprocity and the optional clause

Under Article 36(2):

- The clause is *bilateral*: state A can sue state B at the Court only if
  *both* declarations are in force and the dispute falls within both
  declarations.
- The Court reads reservations strictly. A reservation in either
  declaration can carve the dispute out for both parties (the **Norwegian
  Loans** principle, *France v. Norway*, ICJ Reports 1957 — France had a
  domestic-jurisdiction reservation, and Norway invoked it against
  France).
- Withdrawal/modification of a declaration generally does not affect a
  case already filed (*Nicaragua v. United States*, jurisdictional phase,
  1984).
- Time-of-deposit clauses ("ratione temporis") and "matters arising after
  X date" reservations are common — read the deposit date carefully.

## Common reservation categories

Not exhaustive, but the most frequent shapes:

- **Other-method-of-settlement** reservations — disputes for which the
  parties have agreed to another peaceful settlement method are excluded.
- **Multilateral-treaty (Vandenberg) reservation** — disputes arising
  under a multilateral treaty are excluded unless all affected parties to
  the treaty are also before the Court.
- **Domestic-jurisdiction (self-judging or "Connally") reservations** —
  disputes "essentially within the domestic jurisdiction" of the
  declarant. Modern declarations rarely include the self-judging variant.
- **Commonwealth reservation** — disputes between Commonwealth states
  excluded (e.g., UK declaration historically; cf. India's declaration).
- **Time-bar reservations** — only disputes arising after a specific date,
  or only as to facts arising after a specific date.
- **Subject-matter carve-outs** — e.g., Norway's UNCLOS carve-out (law of
  the sea disputes covered by the special UNCLOS regime).
- **Anti-ambush reservation** — exclusion of disputes brought by states
  whose acceptance was deposited less than X months (often 12) before the
  application.
- **National-security / military-activities reservations** — e.g.,
  Australia's defence-related carve-out.

## Advisory jurisdiction

Only organs and specialized agencies authorised under Charter Art. 96 can
request advisory opinions. The General Assembly and Security Council are
authorised directly; ECOSOC, the Trusteeship Council, and most UN
specialized agencies have been authorised by GA resolutions. See
`jurisdiction organs` for the canonical list and which past opinions each
body has requested.

The Court has discretion (*may* give an opinion) but rarely refuses. The
political nature of the question is not by itself a ground to decline
(*Western Sahara*, 1975; *Israeli Wall*, 2004).
